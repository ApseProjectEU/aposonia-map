What Sound Reveals When We Learn to Listen

Aposonía is a site-specific sound art project investigating underwater acoustic pollution in the Thermaikos Gulf, Thessaloniki. Through five listening stations positioned along the city's waterfront, the work exposes a paradox: this marine area, legally protected under the Natura 2000 network and the Ramsar Convention, suffers from invisible acoustic pollution that remains imperceptible to human senses above water.

As a sound artist working with hydrophone field recordings and psychoacoustic composition, I approach the underwater soundscape as a sonic territory that reveals what our eyes cannot see and what our ears—positioned above water—cannot hear. The hydrophone becomes an instrument of perceptual translation, allowing us entry into the acoustic reality inhabited by marine life.

This project was developed during a Culture Moves Europe residency (March–May 2026), a mobility program funded by the Creative Europe initiative of the European Union and implemented by the Goethe-Institut. Culture Moves Europe supports individual artists and cultural professionals across multiple disciplines, fostering cross-border collaboration and artistic exchange throughout Creative Europe countries.

The Psychoacoustic Foundation: Fight-or-Flight

At the heart of Aposonía lies a psychophysiological mechanism that bridges human experience and marine suffering: the fight-or-flight response. When the amygdala perceives danger through sound alone, it triggers involuntary changes—adrenaline release, increased heart rate, muscle tension. Certain sonic characteristics activate this response before conscious thought intervenes. Conversely, natural water sounds produce the opposite effect: heart rate decreases, cortisol drops, parasympathetic activation creates a meditative state. This installation uses this duality to create embodied interspecies empathy—when you hear acoustic pollution and feel your body tense, you experience in compressed form what marine animals endure continuously, without escape.

I work with sound not as documentation but as a medium of translation between worlds. The hydrophone is my bridge into an acoustic reality we cannot otherwise access. Through this project, I ask: what does it mean to listen across species boundaries? What happens when we experience physiologically, through our own nervous systems, the chronic stress that underwater noise pollution inflicts on marine life? Aposonía is an invitation to hear what has been rendered inaudible by our position above water—and to feel, through psychoacoustic empathy, what it means to inhabit a world where silence has been stolen.
— Gaetano Cappella

About the Project

Gaetano Cappella

Gaetano Cappella is a sound artist working at the intersection of field recording, psychoacoustics, and spatial composition. His practice explores how sound shapes our perception of environment, ecology, and interspecies relationships. Through hydrophone recordings and site-specific installations, he makes audible the acoustic dimensions of environmental change that remain invisible to conventional observation.
